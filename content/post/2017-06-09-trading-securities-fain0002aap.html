Remember: A company that invests in the shares of other companies (i.e., an investor) can use three accounting methods to record these investments: (1) fair value method (for Trading securities [e.g., trading securities can be short-term investments in other companies, usually in small percentages] and Available-for-Sales securities [e.g., available-for-sales securities can be long-term investments in other companies, usually in small percentages]); (2) equity method (for investments in associates [e.g., when the investor has between 25% and 50% of the shares of the investee]); (3) consolidation method (for subsidiaries [e.g., when the investor has over 50% of the shares of the investee]).

Changes in fair values only matter under the fair value method. Changes in the fair value of trading securities create an Unrealized Gain (or Loss) that affects directly the income statement. Changes in the fair value of available-for-sales securities create an Unrealized Gain (or Loss) that is recorded directly in the “Accumulated Other Comprehensive Income OCI - Reserves” section of the Equity.

Question:

At the end of the first year of operations, Company E holds trading securities with a total fair value of $1,150,000. The cost of the trading securities was $1,100,000. The financial statements should show:

  1. An unrealized gain of $50,000 in the Other Comprehensive Income section

  2. An unrealized gain of $50,000 in the Income Statements section

  3. An unrealized loss of $50,000 in the Other Comprehensive Income section

  4. An unrealized loss of $50,000 in the Income Statement section

Solution: Point 2.
